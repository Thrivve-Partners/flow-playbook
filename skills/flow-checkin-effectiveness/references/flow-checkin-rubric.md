# Flow Check-in Effectiveness Rubric

Detailed scoring criteria for evaluating standup transcripts against the Flow Check-In standard (v1.4).

> **Note on rubric versioning:** Analyses produced before 22 April 2026 used the v1.3 rubric. Key changes in v1.4:
> - **Finishing Focus (criterion 2)** is new — rewards the right-to-left walk starting from the last column. Under v1.3, this was not scored; under v1.4, sessions that walk ageing-first (pace-percentile descending or column-by-column from the left) are marked down.
> - **Session Texture (criterion 10)** merges the former "Pattern Recognition & After-Party" and "Overall Health" criteria. It explicitly tests the two-events-shoved-together anti-pattern (chart narration followed by per-person status round).
> - Step numbering has shifted throughout to match v1.4 running order (Step 0, Step 1, Step 2a/2b/2c, Step 3a/3b/3c, Step 4).
>
> The 10-point total is preserved. Scores across rubric versions are broadly comparable for a holistic sense of maturity, but the two new criteria represent stricter scoring on behaviours that v1.3 did not evaluate.

---

## 1. Step 0 — Invisible Work Check (1 point)

**Reference:** Flow Check-In v1.4 — Step 0: Before We Look at the Board

### What to Look For

- Did anyone ask: "Is there anything you're working on that isn't on the board?"
- Was the 30-minute rule mentioned or enforced? ("If it's taking 30 minutes or more of your time, it needs a ticket")
- Did anyone mention work not on the board and create a ticket for it?
- Does the check happen _before_ the board walk begins?

### Scoring

- **1.0** — Invisible work explicitly checked before the board walk; 30-minute rule applied or implicitly understood; produces a response (even if the answer is "no")
- **0.5** — Question asked but at the wrong point (e.g., after the board walk), or mentioned without being systematically checked
- **0.0** — Not addressed

### Evidence to Capture

Direct quotes showing the invisible work check (or absence). Note the position in the session (before or after the board walk).

## 2. Step 1 — Finishing Focus / Right-to-Left Walk (1 point)

**Reference:** Flow Check-In v1.4 — Step 1: What Are We Finishing Today?

The v1.4 standard reframes the opening move of the board walk. The first substantive question is about finishing, not ageing. The walk starts with the last column before done and works right-to-left.

### What to Look For

- Does the board walk open with the last column (ready-for-release, ready-to-merge, or equivalent)?
- Is the framing about finishing? E.g. *"What's in ready-for-release? Can anything cross the line today?"*
- Is one-step-back walked next (test, review, or the final stage before the last column)?
- Is the help-to-land question posed? *"Who's finishing, and what do they need?"*
- Is the team orientation "what can I help land?" rather than "what should I start?"
- **Anti-pattern:** does the walk open with pace-percentile descending, or column-by-column from the left, or oldest-first from the WIA chart? These are the v1.4 anti-patterns explicitly named in the standard.

### Scoring

- **1.0** — Board walk opens with the last column; finishing questions asked explicitly; help-to-land orientation visible; team leaves knowing what they can help finish
- **0.5** — Last column surfaced during the walk but not as the opening move; or the finishing frame is present implicitly (the facilitator knows what's close to done) but is not the anchor of the walk
- **0.0** — Walk opens with pace-percentile descending, oldest-first, or column-by-column from the left; finishing items reached late in the walk (or only when a team member prompts); v1.4 Step 1 anti-pattern present

### Evidence to Capture

- The facilitator's opening move for the walk — quote it verbatim
- Walk order (right-to-left vs ageing-first vs column-first)
- Help-to-land moments (or their absence)
- Whether ready-for-release items received substantive attention or cursory treatment

### Note on common first-pattern

Even strong facilitators default to ageing-first walks because that was the v1.3 instinct and because ageing items look like the "risky" ones. The v1.4 standard reframes this: finishing is the goal, ageing is the context. A facilitator who walks ageing-first is demonstrating v1.3 literacy, not v1.4. Score accordingly and flag for coaching.

## 3. Step 2a — Ageing Items: Three Lenses (1 point)

**Reference:** Flow Check-In v1.4 — Step 2: What's Stuck, Ageing, or Blocked?

The v1.4 standard defines three lenses for identifying ageing items. Each catches something the others miss.

### What to Look For

- **Lens 1 — Overall Age (SLE):** Are items flagged at 50% and 70% of SLE? Is the SLE number stated or clearly understood?
- **Lens 2 — Stage Pace:** Are pace percentiles referenced? Items beyond the 70th pace percentile for their current stage flagged?
- **Lens 3 — Staleness:** Is a staleness threshold applied (x days without movement)? Items beyond the threshold surfaced?

### Scoring

- **1.0** — Two or more lenses used systematically; items flagged at thresholds with appropriate response (flag at 50%, swarm at 70%)
- **0.5** — One lens used (typically SLE or staleness), or age mentioned but not systematically walked; partial threshold awareness
- **0.0** — No age focus; or age is present but only as column-based walk without lens-specific language

### Evidence to Capture

- Which lenses are used (SLE thresholds, pace percentile, staleness flag)
- Threshold references and team responses
- Whether lenses are named or implicit

### Note on scoring interaction with criterion 2

Criterion 2 (Finishing Focus) and criterion 3 (Ageing Three Lenses) can both score well in the same session — a facilitator can walk right-to-left AND apply three lenses when they hit the ageing items in the middle. They can also both score badly if the walk is column-first and ageing isn't lens-aware. The distinction: criterion 2 scores the walk order, criterion 3 scores the ageing analysis when ageing is being discussed.

## 4. Step 2b — Blocker Management (1 point)

**Reference:** Flow Check-In v1.4 — Step 2: Blocked Items

### What to Look For

- Are blockers specifically named (not just "it's blocked")?
- Is someone assigned to own escalation?
- Are blockers properly categorised (Level 1/2/3)?
  - **Level 1:** Blocked, actively seeking resolution, counts against WIP, owned escalation
  - **Level 2:** Resolution expected, escalation window passed, stays on board ageing, requires a stateable resolution path
  - **Level 3:** No stateable resolution, split/cancel/clone
- Is the distinction between "blocked" and "ageing" applied? ("If there's no named impediment, it isn't blocked, it's ageing.")

### Scoring

- **1.0** — Blockers named, owned, escalation clear, levels applied; distinction between blocked and ageing understood
- **0.5** — Blockers mentioned but not owned or categorised; or blockers flagged for tracking without formal classification
- **0.0** — Blockers not addressed or vague ("waiting on X")

### Evidence to Capture

- Blocker naming and ownership examples
- Classification language (Level 1/2/3) or absence
- Blocked vs ageing distinction

---

## 5. Step 2c — Swarming on Ageing Work (1 point)

**Reference:** Flow Check-In v1.4 — Step 2: What's Stuck, Ageing or Blocked?

### What to Look For

- When work isn't moving, does the team offer to swarm?
- At 70% of SLE, does "everything else wait" while the team swarms?
- When someone says "still where I was yesterday", is help offered?
- Are non-moving items treated as flow problems requiring intervention?

### Scoring

- **1.0** — Swarming actively offered when work stalls or reaches 70% SLE; team responds to ageing signals with help, not just status updates
- **0.5** — Swarming recognised but not actively coached; or help offered in some cases but not as a consistent response to ageing signals
- **0.0** — Non-moving work treated as normal status update; "still where I was" accepted without follow-up

### Evidence to Capture

- Swarming offers (or missed opportunities)
- Responses to static work or ageing signals

## 6. Step 3a — WIP Limit Check (1 point)

**Reference:** Flow Check-In v1.4 — Step 3a: Are We Within Our WIP Limits?

### What to Look For

- Did they check: "Are we within our WIP limits?" — a scan across all stages, not just one
- If over: "What needs to finish before anything new starts?"
- If slack: Did they look right on the board before pulling new work?
- "Work as a team on what's already in flight before anyone considers pulling anything new"
- Personal WIP observations (e.g., "you've got three tickets in flight") count as partial

### Scoring

- **1.0** — WIP limits explicitly checked across stages; over/under addressed appropriately
- **0.5** — WIP mentioned but not systematically checked (e.g., one stage only, or personal WIP observation without stage-level scan)
- **0.0** — WIP limits not referenced

### Evidence to Capture

WIP limit discussions — stage-level and personal (or absence)

---

## 7. Step 3b — Pull Criteria & Policies (1 point)

**Reference:** Flow Check-In v1.4 — Step 3b: Are We Using Our Pull Criteria and Policies?

### What to Look For

- Is "finish before pull" applied or mentioned?
- Are pull criteria referenced? ("Work moves because it meets agreed criteria")
- When someone looks for work, is there a check: "Can we finish existing work first?"
- Is the expedite policy mentioned? (one item at a time, swarmed until done)
- Is ticket movement discipline applied? (tickets don't move backwards on the board)

### Scoring

- **1.0** — Pull policy explicitly applied; criteria referenced; finish-before-pull framed as a principle
- **0.5** — Pull policy recognised but not consistently applied; or elements present (e.g., ticket movement discipline) without the full pull criteria framework
- **0.0** — No pull policy evident; work pulled without criteria check

### Evidence to Capture

- Finish-before-pull checks (or missed opportunities)
- Pull criteria applications
- Ticket movement discipline

---

## 8. Step 3c — Replenishment (1 point)

**Reference:** Flow Check-In v1.4 — Step 3c: Does Anyone Need to Replenish?

### What to Look For

- Is there an explicit question: "Does anyone need work?" or "Is anyone looking for work?"
- When someone has capacity, is the next item to pull identified and agreed?
- Is the pull decision based on priority and business value, not self-selection?
- Is backlog readiness discussed when someone needs work but nothing is ready?

### Scoring

- **1.0** — Replenishment question explicitly asked; next item identified based on priority/business value; backlog readiness addressed if needed
- **0.5** — Replenishment happens informally (someone mentions they need work) but the question is not systematically asked by the facilitator; or the pull decision is made without reference to priority
- **0.0** — No replenishment conversation; people left to find their own work or the question is not raised

### Evidence to Capture

- The replenishment question (or absence)
- How the next item to pull is identified (priority-based vs self-selected)
- Backlog readiness discussion (if relevant)

---

## 9. Step 4 — What's Moving Today (1 point)

**Reference:** Flow Check-In v1.4 — Step 4: What's Moving Today?

### What to Look For

- Is the team clear on what progresses today?
- Do people leave knowing exactly what they're working on?
- Is there a structured close — a per-person confirmation of commitments?
- Are commitments gathered into a closing round, not scattered across the session?

### Scoring

- **1.0** — Clear commitments confirmed per person; structured close; everyone leaves knowing what they're working on
- **0.5** — Some clarity but commitments scattered across the session rather than gathered at close; or a closing question asked without per-person answers
- **0.0** — No clear next steps; team leaves unclear; no closing round

### Evidence to Capture

- Closing statements and clarity of commitments
- Per-person confirmation (or absence)

## 10. Session Texture — Integrated Walk + After-Party + Routine Feel (1 point)

**Reference:** Flow Check-In v1.4 — What Good Looks Like; This Is a Team Event

This criterion merges what v1.3 scored as "Pattern Recognition & After-Party" and "Overall Health." It explicitly tests the two-events-shoved-together anti-pattern named in v1.4.

### The v1.4 Anchor

From the v1.4 standard:

> *"One integrated walk, not two events. The Flow Check-in is a conversation that walks the board — as each item is hit, the person working on it contributes, so the signal ('this one's at the 85th percentile') and the commitment ('I'll land the review by lunch') happen in the same beat. A chart narration followed by a per-person status round is two events shoved together: a flow briefing the team listens to, then a standup the team reports in. Neither is the check-in. Walk the board once, start from the right, keep it to five minutes — if something needs more depth, take it to the after-party."*

And from "What Good Looks Like":

> *"The check-in feels routine. The right-hand side of the board empties before the left-hand side fills. Work is moving. Blockers are named early and owned. Items that are ageing get attention before they become problems. The board is an honest picture of where capacity is actually going. The team leaves knowing what they can finish and what they can help land. If the check-in regularly surfaces surprises, or if attention has quietly shifted to oldest-item firefighting while the last column stacks up, then the system needs attention, not a longer meeting."*

### What to Look For

Three distinct signals combine in this criterion:

**A. Integrated walk (not two events)**
- Does the team member on each item contribute as that item is hit, in the same beat?
- Or does the session split into a facilitator-narrated chart walk followed by a per-person status round?
- A session with both phases separately is the v1.4 anti-pattern, even if all structural elements are technically present.

**B. After-party for depth**
- When a topic needs two- or three-person depth, is the after-party model used? *"This needs three of us for five minutes, can you stay on after?"*
- Are systemic patterns noted for flow review (not solved in standup)? *"Note them, name them, and move on."*
- Is the check-in kept to approximately five minutes? If it consistently runs long, is the cause operational conversations that should have been offlined?

**C. Routine feel + team contributing**
- Does the check-in feel habitual rather than chaotic?
- Is the board an honest picture? Is it updated live?
- Does the team (not just the facilitator) surface signals and contribute to the conversation?

### Scoring

- **1.0** — Session is one integrated walk (person contributes as each item is hit); after-party used when depth is needed; patterns noted and deferred; routine feel; team participating not just facilitator narrating
- **0.5** — Integrated walk mostly holds but occasionally fragments; or after-party absent on one clear candidate; or some DM-centricity with limited team contribution; or pattern recognition emerging but not explicit
- **0.0** — Chart narration followed by per-person status round (the v1.4 two-events anti-pattern); or session consistently runs long with depth conversations absorbed; or DM-narrated throughout with team listening rather than contributing; or chaotic with many surprises

### Evidence to Capture

- Session shape — is it one conversation or two halves?
- Per-item contribution pattern (each person speaks as their item is walked, vs. status round at the end)
- After-party usage (or missed opportunities where depth should have been offlined)
- Facilitator vs team contribution balance
- Length and pace signals

### Note on scoring when structural elements are present but texture fragments

A session can score highly on criteria 1–9 (all structural elements present) and still score 0.0 on criterion 10 if the session is the two-events anti-pattern. This is intentional — the v1.4 standard treats texture as a separate dimension from structure. A session that is structurally complete but fragmented is not a v1.4 flow check-in, even if every box is ticked.

This is the criterion that captures the Dean DX3 pattern from 22 April 2026 — structural score 9/10, texture score 0.5/10 (or lower). When you see this split, it's a coaching signal, not a scoring anomaly.

## Interpretation Guide

- **8–10:** Excellent — Flow Check-in standards well embedded, minor refinements only
- **6–7:** Good — Core practices present, needs consistent application
- **4–5:** Developing — Some practices emerging, significant coaching needed
- **2–3:** Early — Few practices present, intensive coaching required
- **0–1:** Not Present — Flow Check-in standards not yet introduced

---

## Common Patterns by Score Range

### Early (2–3/10)
- Column-based walk (not right-to-left, not age-based)
- No invisible work check or one asked mid-session
- Blockers mentioned but not owned
- Swarming happening organically but not coached when work stalls
- No WIP limit checks
- No replenishment question — people find their own work
- Pull policy emerging but not explicit
- Long technical discussions in standup; no after-party
- Session is a facilitator briefing with team listening

### Developing (4–5/10)
- Walk order may be ageing-first (pace-percentile descending); finishing focus not yet adopted
- One ageing lens used (typically SLE or staleness) but not systematically
- Blockers named and owned but not categorised
- Swarming requested in some cases
- Finish-before-pull thinking evident but implicit
- Replenishment informal — team members mention needing work but the facilitator doesn't ask
- Patterns emerging but not explicitly noted
- After-party model used inconsistently
- Session texture: DM-led with some team contribution

### Good (6–7/10)
- Invisible work check asked early, sometimes at the opening
- Walk order may still be ageing-first — v1.4 Step 1 finishing focus not yet the default
- Two or more ageing lenses used
- Blockers named, owned, and beginning to be categorised
- Swarming actively offered when work stalls
- WIP limits checked regularly
- Pull policy explicit in most cases
- Replenishment question asked; pull decisions based on priority
- Patterns noted and deferred appropriately
- After-party model used when needed
- Session texture: mostly integrated, some DM-centricity

### Excellent (8–10/10)
- Invisible work check at the opening, every session
- Walk opens right-to-left from the last column; finishing questions anchor the session
- All three ageing lenses applied systematically when ageing is reached in the walk
- Blockers explicitly categorised with escalation protocol
- Swarming immediate when work doesn't move
- WIP limits checked and acted upon
- Pull criteria consistently applied
- Replenishment is a named step; backlog readiness addressed when needed
- Pattern recognition automatic; after-party model routine
- Session is one integrated conversation — each person contributes as their item is walked
- Routine feel, no surprises, clear structured close
- Team (not just facilitator) reads the chart and surfaces signals
